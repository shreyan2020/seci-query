INT: Can you briefly describe how you approach scientific problems?

SCI: I generally start by trying to understand the structure of uncertainty in the problem. I don't assume the system or human is correct or incorrect initially. Instead, I try to identify what assumptions are being made implicitly. My goal is to model the process by which beliefs are formed and updated, rather than just evaluating the final outcome.

INT: What do you mean by modeling the process instead of the outcome?

SCI: Many systems focus on whether a user made the correct decision, but that ignores how they arrived at that decision. I'm more interested in whether their reasoning process was coherent, whether it followed some rational updating principle, or whether it exhibited systematic biases. Even incorrect outcomes can be informative if the reasoning process is understandable.

INT: How do you decide whether to trust an AI system?

SCI: Trust, for me, is not binary. It's a probabilistic estimate based on observed reliability across contexts. I maintain a mental model of the system's performance characteristics. If the system performs well in one domain, I don't automatically generalize that trust to another domain. I treat each domain as a separate reliability distribution, but I do allow prior experience to influence my expectations slightly.

INT: So prior experience matters?

SCI: Yes, but it should not dominate new evidence. One of the biggest problems I see is belief inertia. People either trust too much or distrust too much based on early interactions. I try to update beliefs gradually, in proportion to the strength and relevance of evidence.

INT: What do you do when an AI system makes an unexpected error?

SCI: The first thing I do is check whether the error is actually surprising under my current model of the system. Sometimes what appears to be an error is consistent with the system's limitations. If it is genuinely unexpected, then I revise my internal model of the system's capabilities. I try not to overreact to single failures, but I also don't ignore systematic patterns.

INT: Do you rely on explanations provided by AI systems?

SCI: I use explanations primarily to update my model of the system, not necessarily to justify the specific output. Explanations can help me identify when the system is reasoning in a way that aligns with my expectations versus when it is relying on spurious patterns. However, I remain cautious because explanations themselves can be unreliable.

INT: How do you handle conflicting information?

SCI: I try to preserve both hypotheses until additional evidence resolves the conflict. I avoid prematurely collapsing uncertainty. It's important to maintain multiple candidate models rather than committing too early.

INT: Do you prefer simple models or complex models?

SCI: I prefer models that are as simple as possible while still capturing the essential structure of the problem. Complexity is justified only if it provides meaningful explanatory power. Otherwise, it increases the risk of overfitting and reduces interpretability.

INT: What role does human judgment play in AI-assisted decision-making?

SCI: Human judgment is essential, especially when decisions involve incomplete information or ethical considerations. AI systems are useful as tools for expanding the hypothesis space, but they should not be treated as authoritative sources of truth.

INT: How do you evaluate whether a system is useful?

SCI: I evaluate systems based on how they change the user's belief state and decision process, not just task performance. A system that helps users form more accurate mental models is more valuable than one that occasionally produces correct answers without improving understanding.

INT: Do you ever ignore AI recommendations?

SCI: Yes, especially when the recommendation conflicts with strong prior knowledge or when the system has shown unreliable behavior in similar contexts. However, I remain open to revising my position if new evidence emerges.

INT: How do you approach designing AI systems?

SCI: I focus on making the system's behavior predictable and understandable. Users should be able to form accurate mental models of the system. This reduces both overtrust and undertrust.

INT: What is one common mistake people make when using AI?

SCI: They assume consistency. AI systems can behave differently depending on subtle changes in input or context. Users need to continuously recalibrate their expectations.

INT: How do you personally deal with uncertainty?

SCI: I try to make uncertainty explicit. Instead of forcing a decision prematurely, I represent uncertainty formally, either probabilistically or through alternative hypotheses. This allows me to update beliefs systematically.

INT: What motivates your research?

SCI: I'm interested in understanding the interaction between humans and intelligent systems as a dynamic process. I want to design systems that support better reasoning rather than replacing human reasoning.

INT: Do your views ever change?

SCI: Yes, and I consider that a positive outcome. If my views never change, it means I'm not learning. I try to remain open to updating my beliefs whenever the evidence justifies it.

INT: How do you validate your ideas?

SCI: I prefer empirical validation through controlled experiments. Theoretical models are useful, but they must be tested against observed behavior.

INT: How do you balance innovation and rigor?

SCI: Innovation should not come at the expense of validity. I try to explore new ideas, but I also ensure they can be evaluated systematically.

INT: How do you want AI systems to evolve?

SCI: I want AI systems to become more transparent, more predictable, and better aligned with human reasoning processes. The goal should be to augment human cognition, not replace it.

INT: Is there anything else you'd like to add?

SCI: Yes. One important principle is that systems should make it easier for users to understand when they might be wrong. Helping users detect and correct errors is more valuable than simply providing answers.
